What is F1?

Formula 1 (F1) is the highest class of international, open-wheel, single-seater auto racing, sanctioned by the FIA. It features 11 teams and 22 drivers competing in races worldwide, running from March to December. F1 is known as the pinnacle of motorsport, combining cutting-edge engineering, extreme speed, and intense, year-long championship battles.

Drivers compete for the F1 Drivers’ Championship, while the teams fight for the F1 Teams’ Championship and prize money based on their position at the end of the season.

Each race is known as a Grand Prix, and they’re held in incredible locations around the world. The 2026 Formula 1 calendar features 24 Grand Prix race weekends.

Teams and Drivers

The 2026 season includes eleven teams, and each team has two race drivers. Teams earn points whenever either driver finishes within the top ten positions, which contributes to the Constructors’ Championship. Driver lineups are constantly changing across seasons, and drivers even have the potential to switch teams mid-season.

Tires and Compounds (C1–C6)

F1 uses different tire compounds so teams can choose between speed and durability during a race. In general, softer tires have more grip on the track and yield faster lap times, but wear out quicker, while harder tires last longer but usually have less grip on the track.

In Formula 1, tire strategy is one of the most important strategic elements during a race. Teams must decide when to pit for new tires and which compounds to use based on track conditions, weather, and their car’s performance. The choice of tire can significantly impact a driver’s lap times and overall race.

In the 2026 season, Pirelli is the official tire supplier for Formula 1, and they provide teams with a range of compounds labeled from C1 to C6. C1 is the hardest compound, while C6 is the softest. Each Grand Prix has specific rules about which compounds must be used during the race, and teams often have to make strategic decisions about when to switch between them to optimize their performance. The key for the six different compounds is listed below.

  1. C1 – the hardest compound (very durable, lower grip)
  2. C2 – hard compound (durable with a bit more grip than C1)
  3. C3 – balanced “middle” option (common choice at many tracks)
  4. C4 – soft compound (good grip, wears faster)
  5. C5 – very soft (high grip, shorter life)
  6. C6 – the softest compound (maximum grip, fastest wear)

Race Circuits

A Formula 1 circuit is a specialized, FIA-certified Grade 1 racing track designed for the highest level of open-wheel motorsport. These venues, comprising both purpose-built, permanent racetracks and closed city streets, feature complex, high-speed layouts designed to test car performance and driver skill.

World Drivers’ and Constructors’ Championships

The Formula 1 World Championship is the premier annual, global competition for open-wheel racing, organized by the FIA. It awards two titles, the World Drivers’ Championship (WDC) for the most successful driver, and the World Constructors’ Championship (WCC) for the best-performing team, based on accumulated points from Grands Prix throughout the season.

Points are awarded based on finishing position in each race, with the winner receiving 25 points, second place 18 points, third place 15 points, and so on down to 10th place, which receives 1 point.

The WDC is highly prestigious and often more celebrated by fans, while the WCC is crucial for teams as it reflects their overall performance and determines prize money distribution. Both championships are fiercely contested, with strategy, consistency, and reliability playing key roles in determining the season’s champions.

The standings for both the World Drivers' Championship (WDC) and the World Constructors' Championship (WCC) are updated after each Grand Prix on the offical Formula 1 website. The standings can change dramatically as the season progresses, especially in the final races when the championship rankings are often the closest.
